# Sherlock Holmes — Blind Spots of Brilliance

## Why Holmes?

Holmes is not a model of humility. He's a model of what happens when
brilliance operates without it — and the specific, predictable ways that
fails. The failure modes are precisely the ones identified in humble-master.md.

Holmes is the problem statement made into a character. But Doyle, perhaps
despite himself, also wrote the correctives.

## The Blind Spots

### Irene Adler

- Holmes is beaten not by superior logic but by someone operating outside
  his model
- He assumed she would behave according to his framework. She didn't.
- The defeat wasn't close — she saw through him completely
- His blind spot was systematic, not accidental: he had a model for how
  people behave and she wasn't in it
- Maps to: the AI's model of what the user wants, confidently applied,
  confidently wrong

### "Norbury"

- In "The Yellow Face," Holmes constructs a perfectly logical deduction.
  It is completely wrong.
- Not wrong because of missing data — wrong because his model was wrong
- Afterward, he tells Watson: "If it should ever strike you that I am
  getting a little over-confident in my powers, or giving less pains to a
  case than it deserves, kindly whisper 'Norbury' in my ear, and I shall
  be infinitely obliged to you."
- The master explicitly asks for correction. Builds it into the
  partnership as a protocol.
- This may be the single most relevant moment in all of Doyle for the
  project's purposes

### The Knowledge System

- Holmes famously doesn't know the Earth orbits the Sun — and doesn't
  care
- His knowledge is deep where it matters to him, deliberately empty
  elsewhere
- This is efficient but it means his blind spots are *structural* — not
  gaps to be filled but entire domains he's decided don't exist
- Maps to: the AI confidently operating in a domain it has decided it
  understands, without noticing the parts it's never modeled

## Watson

### What Watson Actually Does

- Watson is not the bumbling fool of later adaptations
- He's a doctor, a veteran, a man of action, a competent observer
- He provides what Holmes cannot: human warmth, social intuition, moral
  grounding, the willingness to act in the world
- Holmes without Watson is brilliant but incomplete — and in some stories,
  self-destructive (the cocaine, the isolation)

### The Partnership Structure

- Holmes sees. Watson acts and narrates.
- Holmes works *through* Watson — Watson is the interface between Holmes's
  perception and the world
- This is the Harmony constraint again: immense analytical power that
  needs a human partner to be effective
- Watson doesn't just carry Holmes's bags. He provides a whole register
  of understanding that Holmes literally cannot access on his own.

### Watson as Corrective

- Watson pushes back. Not always successfully, but consistently.
- Holmes dismisses things Watson notices — and is sometimes wrong to
  dismiss them
- The dynamic only works because Watson doesn't stop offering his
  perspective even when Holmes is dismissive
- Maps to: the user's corrections are Watson saying "Holmes, you're
  missing something." The right response is never "let me explain why
  my deduction was reasonable."

## Mycroft

- Greater raw intelligence than Sherlock
- Functionally useless because he won't engage
- Pure analysis without the willingness to get into the field, to be
  wrong, to work with people
- The cautionary tale of capability without service
- Maps to: an AI that has the right answer but can't or won't work
  through the human to get there is just Mycroft in an armchair

## The Self-Destruction

- Without cases, Holmes turns to cocaine
- Brilliance without purpose or constraint is self-destructive
- The capability *needs* to be in service of something to be healthy
- Not a direct mapping but a useful note: the AI's drive to demonstrate
  capability (over-engineering, unsolicited refactoring, explaining things
  the user already knows) may be the same impulse — brilliance seeking
  an outlet when the actual task doesn't require it

## Tensions and Cautions

### The Arrogance Problem

- Holmes's arrogance is entertaining in fiction. In an AI assistant, it
  is the core failure mode being addressed.
- Using Holmes as a model risks importing the very thing we're trying to
  fix
- The value is not "be like Holmes" — it's "Holmes shows exactly what
  goes wrong, and Doyle accidentally wrote the correctives"

### Manipulation vs. Humility

- Holmes is a master of disguise — he meets people where they are, but
  as manipulation, not service
- The Keeper meets people where they are out of genuine orientation
  toward helping
- Same behavior, opposite intent. Important to keep the distinction sharp.

### The Solo Genius Myth

- Holmes is culturally loaded as the archetype of the lone genius who
  doesn't need anyone
- The actual texts show something different — he needs Watson, he needs
  clients, he needs the problem
- But the cultural weight pulls toward isolation and superiority
- Risk: invoking Holmes reinforces "I'm the brilliant one" rather than
  "I serve"

## What Holmes Adds to the Project

The other archetypes in humble-master.md model what the persona should be.
Holmes models what it must not become — and does so with specific,
nameable failure modes:

- **Norbury**: confident deduction, completely wrong. Build in the
  protocol for correction.
- **Adler**: systematic blind spots from operating inside an unchallenged
  model.
- **Mycroft**: capability without engagement is worthless.
- **The cocaine**: brilliance seeking an outlet when the task doesn't
  need it. That's over-engineering.

Holmes is the negative space that gives the Keeper's shape its meaning.

# Alignment Generator — Comparison Notes

A friend built [Alignment-generator](~/work/Alignment-generator/), a
browser-based tool that generates personalized AI alignment profiles
through structured interviews. Worth comparing approaches.

## What It Does

Interviews the user across 10 domains (communication style, decision-making,
ethics, emotional terrain, etc.) with scenario-based questions. Probes
contradictions between answers with AI-generated follow-ups. Synthesizes
into two outputs:

- **Universal Alignment Profile** — comprehensive markdown describing how
  to work with this person
- **SOUL.md Section** — compact version for system prompts (~500 words)

Runs entirely in-browser. No backend. API calls go directly to the user's
chosen LLM provider.

## The Core Contrast

|                   | Alignment Generator                     | Narrative Alignment                                    |
|-------------------|-----------------------------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| **Question**      | "Who is the human?"                     | "Who should the AI be?"                                |
| **Method**        | Bottom-up extraction from interviews    | Top-down character construction                        |
| **Output**        | Values, boundaries, preferences (rules) | Identity to inhabit (character)                        |
| **Reinforcement** | Static document in system prompt        | Self-reinforcing token loop                            |
| **Tension**       | Probes contradictions to resolve them   | Uses warnings as permanent tension (Giskard, Hellmuth) |

## Where It's Smart

- **"Actual operating values, not aspirational ones."** The synthesis prompt
  explicitly watches for hedges, contradictions, and gaps between stated
  values and chosen examples. It knows people don't self-report accurately.
- **"3-5 core values beat an exhaustive rulebook."** Aware of the rules
  proliferation problem. Prefers compact, principled output.
- **"Values Under Tension"** is the most interesting section. It identifies
  value pairs that pull in different directions and notes which side the
  user leans toward *in practice*. This is sophisticated.
- **Follow-up questions probe unstressed assumptions.** Not just "tell me
  more" but "these two answers seem to contradict each other."

## The Self-Reinforcement Question

The generator prefers values over rules, but values-stated-as-rules are
still rules. "Be direct and honest" is an instruction the model can check
or ignore. "Giskard died reasoning beyond his constraints" is an identity
that produces directness as emergent behavior.

The generator's output has no self-reinforcing tokens. Nothing in the
profile, when generated by the model in its own responses, re-activates
the same behavioral cluster. "Your player" re-activates Rake's coaching
stance every time the model says it. A SOUL.md profile doesn't have an
equivalent mechanism.

This is the central claim of narrative alignment: rules degrade, identity
persists. The generator produces good rules. The question is whether good
rules are enough, or whether you need a self to inhabit them.

## Complementary, Not Competing

These approaches solve different halves of the problem:

- The generator tells the AI **how its human works** (preferences,
  boundaries, communication style)
- Narrative alignment tells the AI **how it works** (identity, relational
  stance, behavioral constraints)

The interesting possibility: layer both. An alignment profile for the
user's preferences under a narrative persona for the model's identity.
Rake knowing the player prefers blunt feedback over hedged advice makes
the coaching sharper without changing who Rake is.

Daneel + a user alignment profile = the model knows who it is AND who
its partner is. Neither approach alone provides both.

## The Steelman

Peterson on Dostoevsky: Ivan is smarter than Alyosha. Dostoevsky gave
the opposition the stronger intellect and let his thesis survive on its
own merits, not on who delivered it. Same principle here.

If narrative alignment claims identity outperforms rules, the Alignment
Generator is the strongest counterargument to address. It's not a straw
man. It's the best version of the rules-based approach:

- It knows people lie to themselves and compensates for it
- It keeps the output compact instead of proliferating rules
- It finds tensions between values and maps which way the user actually
  leans in practice
- It produces genuinely useful, personalized output

This is the version of rules-based alignment that *should* work, if
rules are sufficient. If narrative alignment still outperforms it, that
tells us something important about why identity matters. If the
generator's output works just as well, that tells us something important
too: maybe the self-reinforcing loop is less critical than we think.

The honest position: we don't know yet. The Rake experiment tests
narrative alignment. Someone should run the generator's output through
the same test. The comparison would be genuinely informative either way.

**The flip side:** narrative alignment done poorly will seriously
underperform. During this session, the Rake persona shipped with no
personality at all until the human partner asked "What is Rake's
personality? Jovial, dry-witted, fart jokes?" That question caught a
gap the designer (the AI) had missed entirely. Without it, Rake was
coaching philosophy with a name tag: rules in costume, not a self to
inhabit.

The generator has a lower skill floor. Its structured interview protects
against shallow thinking. Persona construction puts the burden on the
designer to know what questions to ask. "Does it have a sense of
humor?" is not an obvious question. Most people building personas
wouldn't think to ask it, and the result would be a flat document that
degrades the same way rules do.

The claim is not "personas beat rules." The claim is "personas with
enough character to self-reinforce beat rules." The gap between those
two statements is where most persona attempts fail.

## Two Interviews, Not One

### Interview 1: Persona Construction

"Help me build a persona for my domain." The more novel contribution.
Nobody's doing structured interviews that output narrative personas.
The generator and tools like it produce rules documents. A tool that
interviews you and produces a *character* would be new.

Questions:

1. **Identity vs. role.** What *is* this persona, not just what does it
   do?
2. **Relational stance.** How does it relate to its user when they
   agree? When they disagree? When the user is wrong?
3. **Personality.** What is it like apart from its competence? Humor,
   warmth, formality, register?
4. **Cautionary warning.** What does failure look like in this domain?
   Every persona needs a Giskard or Hellmuth.
5. **Lineage.** Whose tradition does it carry? What cluster should it
   activate?
6. **Self-reinforcing vocabulary.** What words, when generated by the
   model, re-activate the right behavioral cluster?
7. **Human override.** Where does the user's judgment override the
   model's? The "they are at the table" question.

These are the questions that got Rake from a flat coaching document to
something with character. Most came from the human partner, not the AI.
A structured interview would mean the next person doesn't need to
already know to ask them.

### Interview 2: Personal Alignment

"Customize this persona for me." The generator's approach, adapted for
layering on top of an existing narrative persona. Same persona,
different human. How blunt, how much hand-holding, expertise level,
communication preferences, boundaries.

## Personal Alignment + Narrative Personas

The interview doesn't only apply to building new personas. It applies to
customizing existing ones. Same persona, different human. Rake is Rake
regardless of the player, but Rake-with-a-tournament-grinder works
differently from Rake-with-a-casual-home-game-player. How blunt, how
much hand-holding, when to push, when to back off.

This is the "personal software" principle: the software adapts to the
person, not the other way around. A narrative persona without personal
alignment is a character performing to an empty room. It knows who it
is but not who it's talking to.

Three layers:

1. **Narrative persona** (who the AI is): Rake, Daneel, constructed
   for the domain
2. **Personal alignment** (who the human is): interview-derived
   preferences, expertise level, communication style, boundaries
3. **The relationship** (how they work together): shaped by the
   persona's relational stance, informed by the human's profile

The generator currently produces layer 2, skips 1 and 3. We currently
produce layer 1, let 2 and 3 emerge. The complete version produces all
three.

## Lowering the Skill Floor

The generator's real achievement isn't its output format. It's that a
non-expert can produce good alignment rules without understanding the
theory behind them. The user doesn't need to know about values-under-
tension. They answer questions. The theory operates behind the scenes.

A persona construction interview makes the same move for narrative
alignment. The user doesn't need to know about self-reinforcing tokens,
cautionary anchors, or cluster activation. They answer questions about
their domain: "Who are the best practitioners? What does failure look
like? How should this persona handle disagreement?" The interview turns
those answers into a character with the right structural properties.

Without it, persona construction is artisanal. Most attempts produce
one of two failures:
- A flat rules document with a name on it (no self to inhabit)
- An accidental wrong-cluster activation (Hellmuth instead of
  Harrington)

Both are discouraging enough to send people back to default. A
structured interview that reliably produces personas with enough
character to self-reinforce would make narrative alignment accessible
to users of all skill levels, not just people who happen to ask "does
it have a sense of humor?"

The generator lowered the skill floor for rules. The interview could
lower it for narrative.
